** Our explanation for veracity score **
Research indicates that strict voter ID laws do not significantly reduce voter fraud cases. A study by Cantoni and Pons found no significant impact of such laws on actual or perceived voter fraud. ([vox.com](https://www.vox.com/policy-and-politics/2019/2/21/18230009/voter-id-laws-fraud-turnout-study-research)) Additionally, voter fraud is extremely rare in the U.S., making the effect of voter ID laws on reducing fraud minimal. ([brennancenter.org](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/policy-brief-voter-identification))

** Our explanation for veracity score **
There is no evidence of a National Bureau of Economic Research study linking Canada's and Germany's election security to robust ID requirements. While both countries have high voter registration rates, their systems differ: Germany uses automatic registration through civil registries, and Canada employs data sharing among agencies. Neither country's election security is solely attributed to ID requirements. ([responsivegov.org](https://responsivegov.org/research/comparative-voter-registration-lessons-from-abroad-for-improving-access-and-accuracy-in-the-united-states/))

** Our explanation for veracity score **
The claim that the Electoral Reform Society (ERS) has shown that providing free ID and ensuring adequate voter education can mitigate reduced turnout due to voter ID laws is inaccurate. The ERS has expressed concerns that mandatory voter ID could disenfranchise legitimate voters, particularly those lacking the required identification. They argue that such measures pose risks to a free and fair franchise in the UK. There is no evidence from the ERS indicating that free ID and voter education effectively counteract potential declines in voter turnout caused by voter ID laws.
