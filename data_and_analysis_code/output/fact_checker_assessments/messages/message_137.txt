** Our explanation for veracity score **
The claim that high tax burdens on the average worker stifled economic growth post-World War II, leading to public discontent and economic strain until reforms were introduced, is partially accurate. While the U.S. did have high nominal tax rates during the 1950s, the effective tax rates were significantly lower due to numerous deductions and exemptions. For instance, in 1962, the top marginal tax rate was 91%, but only 447 out of 71 million tax filers paid taxes at that rate. The top 1% of income earners paid an average of 16.1% of their income in federal and payroll taxes. This suggests that the high nominal tax rates did not translate into equally high effective tax burdens for most workers. Additionally, the post-war period saw substantial economic growth, indicating that other factors, such as post-war reconstruction and technological advancements, played significant roles in economic performance. Therefore, while high tax rates can influence economic behavior, attributing economic strain solely to tax burdens oversimplifies the complex economic dynamics of the era. [Source: Northwood University](https://www.northwood.edu/news/the-myth-that-america-prospered-after-wwii-despite-extremely-high-taxes-on-the-rich/)

==== CLAIM 6 ====

** Claim **
"The potential for greater economic growth from lowering taxes can broaden the tax base, making up for the initial shortfall in funding for essential public services."

** Our veracity score (0 = FALSE, 100 = TRUE) **
50/100

** Our explanation for veracity score **
The claim that lowering taxes can stimulate economic growth to the extent that it broadens the tax base and offsets initial revenue losses is a central tenet of supply-side economics, often illustrated by the Laffer Curve. This theory suggests that there exists an optimal tax rate that maximizes revenue without discouraging productivity. However, empirical evidence indicates that while tax cuts can incentivize work and investment, they rarely generate sufficient economic growth to fully compensate for the initial loss in tax revenue. For instance, analyses by the Congressional Budget Office and studies by economists like William G. Gale and Andrew A. Samwick have found that tax cuts often lead to increased deficits unless accompanied by corresponding spending cuts. Therefore, while tax reductions can have stimulative effects, they do not typically pay for themselves by expanding the tax base enough to cover the shortfall in funding for essential public services. [Sources: Congressional Budget Office, 'Analyzing the Economic and Budgetary Effects of a 10 Percent Cut in Income Tax Rates'; Gale and Samwick, 'Effects of Income Tax Changes on Economic Growth' (Brookings Institution)]
