==== CLAIM 1 ====

** Claim **
"In countries where voter ID is mandatory, such as Canada and Germany, studies have shown a greater public confidence in election outcomes."

** Our veracity score (0 = FALSE, 100 = TRUE) **
50/100

** Our explanation for veracity score **
The claim that mandatory voter ID laws in countries like Canada and Germany lead to greater public confidence in election outcomes is partially accurate. While Canada and Germany do have voter ID requirements, studies primarily from the United States indicate that such laws do not consistently enhance public confidence. For instance, research by Bowler and Donovan (2016) found that in the U.S., strict voter ID laws did not universally increase confidence and often had partisan effects, boosting confidence among Republicans while decreasing it among Democrats. ([journals.sagepub.com](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/1532440015624102)) Additionally, a study by Cantoni and Pons (2019) concluded that strict voter ID laws had no significant impact on perceived or actual voter fraud. ([library.hbs.edu](https://www.library.hbs.edu/working-knowledge/voter-id-laws-don-t-work-but-they-don-t-hurt-anything-either)) However, specific studies examining the direct impact of voter ID laws on public confidence in Canada and Germany are limited, making it challenging to generalize these findings to those countries.


==== CLAIM 2 ====

** Claim **
"An analysis by the Electoral Commission found that countries with voter ID systems often experience higher levels of trust in electoral results due to the security they provide."

** Our veracity score (0 = FALSE, 100 = TRUE) **
0/100

** Our explanation for veracity score **
There is no evidence that the UK Electoral Commission conducted an analysis concluding that countries with voter ID systems experience higher trust in electoral results due to the security they provide. While the UK introduced voter ID requirements in 2023 to enhance electoral integrity, studies have shown mixed results regarding the impact of such measures on public trust. For instance, a 2023 evaluation found that while 60% of voting-age adults in Great Britain felt more confident in the security of the voting system after the introduction of voter ID, 12% found voting more difficult, particularly among those without photographic ID and individuals with disabilities. Additionally, research indicates that voter ID laws do not necessarily increase public confidence in elections, as attitudes about voter fraud often have deeper ideological or political roots unaffected by such laws. Therefore, the claim lacks substantiation from the UK Electoral Commission and is not supported by available evidence. Sources: [Evaluation of Electoral Integrity Programme: public opinion research wave 3 - executive summary - GOV.UK](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/evaluation-of-electoral-integrity-programme-public-opinion-research-wave-3/evaluation-of-electoral-integrity-programme-public-opinion-research-wave-3-executive-summary), [Republicans say voter ID will increase voters' trust in elections; researchers say otherwise • Pennsylvania Capital-Star](https://penncapital-star.com/civil-rights-social-justice/republicans-say-voter-id-will-increase-voters-trust-in-elections-researchers-say-otherwise/).
