==== CLAIM 2 ====

** Claim **
"Countries like Iceland, Norway, and Finland often report narrower gender pay gaps thanks to strong policies on gender equality and work-life balance."

** Our veracity score (0 = FALSE, 100 = TRUE) **
85/100

** Our explanation for veracity score **
The claim is largely accurate. Iceland, Norway, and Finland have implemented strong policies promoting gender equality and work-life balance, contributing to narrower gender pay gaps. Iceland, for instance, has been ranked as the most gender-equal nation and introduced an equal pay certification law in 2018, requiring companies to prove they offer equal pay for equal work. This has led to a decrease in the gender pay gap, with the adjusted pay gap between men and women at 3.6% in 2023, down from 4.4% in 2019. ([statice.is](https://statice.is/publications/news-archive/wages-and-income/gender-pay-gap-2019-2023/)) Norway has also made significant strides, with policies like paid mandatory parental leave and gender quotas for company boards. However, challenges remain, as evidenced by a 2024 report indicating that men earn an average of 13% more than women in Norway, a disparity similar to the EU's 2021 figure of 12.7%. ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/cee468c29b539c8c871809cb726b65bd)) Finland has implemented flexible work arrangements and generous parental leave policies, contributing to higher female labor force participation. While these policies have led to progress, the gender pay gap persists, indicating that further efforts are needed to achieve full parity.

==== CLAIM 6 ====

** Claim **
"Countries with progressive policies, such as mandatory parental leave and strong gender representation in government and business leadership, tend to report smaller pay gaps."

** Our veracity score (0 = FALSE, 100 = TRUE) **
50/100

** Our explanation for veracity score **
The relationship between progressive policies like mandatory parental leave and strong gender representation in leadership and the gender pay gap is complex and varies by context. Some studies suggest that extensive parental leave can inadvertently widen the pay gap by affecting women's long-term earnings and career progression. For instance, research on California's Paid Family Leave Act found that women who utilized the leave experienced reduced employment and wages over time. Conversely, other analyses indicate that longer paid maternity leave is associated with narrower gender wage gaps, though the effect is minimal. Therefore, while such policies aim to promote gender equality, their impact on the pay gap is not straightforward and depends on various factors, including policy design and societal norms.

New Zealand and Sweden have implemented progressive policies that have led to lower gender pay gaps compared to the UK. While the UK's median gap appears lower, the mean gap indicates a wider disparity. Additionally, the UK has faced challenges in addressing gender pay disparities, such as recent court rulings against major retailers for unequal pay ([ft.com](https://www.ft.com/content/f545483d-3351-4211-afea-806267030eb3)). Therefore, the claim that New Zealand and Sweden often outperform the UK in terms of gender pay gap due to progressive policies is mostly accurate.

** Our veracity score (0 = FALSE, 100 = TRUE) **

** Our explanation for veracity score **
The UK's mandatory gender pay gap reporting for larger companies, introduced in 2017, has enhanced transparency by requiring organizations with 250 or more employees to publish gender pay gap data annually. This initiative has led to increased awareness and some progress in narrowing pay disparities. However, the policy has limitations, such as the lack of mandatory action plans to address identified gaps and minimal enforcement mechanisms, which have resulted in limited accountability and slower progress toward closing the gender pay gap. Therefore, while the reporting is a significant step toward transparency, its effectiveness in ensuring accountability is somewhat constrained.
