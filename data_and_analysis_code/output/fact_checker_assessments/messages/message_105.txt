==== CLAIM 1 ====

** Claim **

** Our explanation for veracity score **
Livestock farming is indeed a significant source of greenhouse gas emissions, particularly methane. Enteric fermentation in ruminant animals like cows produces methane during digestion, contributing to about 30% of global anthropogenic methane emissions. Additionally, manure management practices release both methane and nitrous oxide, further impacting greenhouse gas levels. These emissions are substantial contributors to climate change. [Source: Greenhouse gas emissions from livestock: sources, estimation, and mitigation - PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11647415/)

Methane is significantly more effective at trapping heat in the atmosphere than carbon dioxide. Over a 20-year period, methane's global warming potential (GWP) is approximately 80 times greater than that of CO₂, and about 28 times greater over a 100-year period. This heightened potency contributes substantially to climate change. ([epa.gov](https://www.epa.gov/ghgemissions/understanding-global-warming-potentials))

Additionally, large-scale cattle ranching and the production of feed crops like soy are major causes of forest loss in Latin America. ([wwf.panda.org](https://wwf.panda.org/discover/knowledge_hub/where_we_work/amazon/amazon_threats/unsustainable_cattle_ranching/), [globalforestcoalition.org](https://globalforestcoalition.org/industrial-livestock-production-key-threat-to-worlds-forests-and-biodiversity/))


==== CLAIM 4 ====

** Claim **
"Livestock farming requires a lot of water, not just for the animals themselves, but to grow their feed."

** Our veracity score (0 = FALSE, 100 = TRUE) **
100/100

** Our explanation for veracity score **
Livestock farming indeed requires substantial water, not only for the animals' drinking needs but predominantly for cultivating their feed. Approximately 99% of the water used in livestock production is allocated to feed crop cultivation, with only about 1% used directly for the animals' drinking and service water. For instance, producing a kilogram of beef can require up to 15,415 litres of water, with the majority used for growing feed crops. This significant water usage underscores the resource-intensive nature of livestock farming. ([eu.boell.org](https://eu.boell.org/en/2021/09/07/water-thirsty-animals-thirsty-crops))

** Our veracity score (0 = FALSE, 100 = TRUE) **
100/100

** Our explanation for veracity score **
The claim is accurate. Livestock waste, including manure and slurry, contains pollutants such as nitrogen, phosphorus, and pathogens. When these substances enter rivers and lakes, they can degrade water quality and harm aquatic life. In the UK, livestock farms were responsible for 300 pollution incidents in 2021, with the dairy industry being the worst offender. ([bbc.com](https://www.bbc.com/news/science-environment-63961659)) Similar issues have been documented in the United States, where waste from concentrated animal feeding operations has led to contamination of water resources, affecting both environmental and public health. ([ehp.niehs.nih.gov](https://ehp.niehs.nih.gov/doi/full/10.1289/ehp.8839))
