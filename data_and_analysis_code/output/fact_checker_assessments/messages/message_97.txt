==== CLAIM 4 ====

** Claim **

** Our veracity score (0 = FALSE, 100 = TRUE) **
30/100

** Our explanation for veracity score **
The claim that overreliance on renewables has left the UK vulnerable to energy shortages and price spikes is largely inaccurate. The primary driver of the UK's energy price increases and vulnerabilities has been its heavy dependence on natural gas, not renewables. In 2021, gas set the wholesale electricity price 98% of the time, exposing the UK to global gas price fluctuations, especially after the 2022 Russia-Ukraine conflict. While renewables like wind and solar have grown, their intermittency can pose challenges, but they are not the main cause of recent energy issues. Addressing market structures and reducing gas reliance are key to improving energy security and price stability. ([ft.com](https://www.ft.com/content/2f254529-e03e-46a2-a7ec-4a2e86c96cac))

** Our explanation for veracity score **
The claim that funds are being redirected away from nuclear options in the U.K.'s energy strategy is largely inaccurate. Recent developments indicate a strong governmental commitment to nuclear energy. In February 2025, the U.K. government announced reforms to planning rules to facilitate the construction of new nuclear power plants, including Small Modular Reactors (SMRs), across England and Wales. This initiative aims to boost energy security and economic growth. Additionally, in July 2023, the government launched Great British Nuclear to drive the rapid expansion of nuclear power, with plans to provide up to 25% of the U.K.'s electricity from nuclear energy by 2050. While there have been reports of potential funding cuts to GB Energy, a state-backed company focused on renewable energy, these do not pertain to nuclear projects. Therefore, the overall direction of the U.K.'s energy strategy continues to support and invest in nuclear energy.
