==== CLAIM 1 ====

** Claim **
"Utilizing CCS at existing coal and gas-fired power plants in the U.K. can substantially cut emissions while maintaining a stable energy supply."

** Our veracity score (0 = FALSE, 100 = TRUE) **
60/100

** Our explanation for veracity score **
Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS) can reduce emissions from coal and gas-fired power plants in the UK, but its effectiveness is debated. While CCS has the potential to capture up to 90% of CO₂ emissions, real-world applications often achieve lower reductions due to factors like upstream methane emissions and energy penalties associated with the capture process. Additionally, the high costs and unproven large-scale deployment raise concerns about its feasibility and impact on energy supply stability. Critics argue that reliance on CCS may prolong fossil fuel dependence and divert resources from renewable energy investments. Therefore, while CCS can contribute to emission reductions, its substantial impact and role in maintaining a stable energy supply in the UK remain uncertain. ** Our veracity score (0 = FALSE, 100 = TRUE) **

** Our explanation for veracity score **
The claim that Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS) can capture CO₂ emissions from natural gas plants in the UK, supporting a cleaner transition fuel in the shift from coal, is largely accurate. Several projects in the UK are actively developing CCS technology for natural gas power plants. For instance, the Net Zero Teesside Power project aims to build a 742 MW gas-fired power plant with carbon capture, set to capture approximately 2 million tonnes of CO₂ annually upon its expected operation in 2028. Similarly, the Keadby 3 project in North Lincolnshire plans to capture up to 1.5 million tonnes of CO₂ per year from a 910 MW gas-fired power station. These initiatives align with the UK's goal to decarbonize its power sector and transition from coal to cleaner energy sources. However, while these projects demonstrate the feasibility of CCS in natural gas plants, the technology is still in the development and early deployment stages, and its widespread implementation and long-term effectiveness remain to be fully realized. Therefore, the claim is highly credible but not yet fully substantiated across the entire sector.

This approach is integral to the UK's plan to develop a low-carbon hydrogen sector, aiming for 10GW of low-carbon hydrogen production capacity by 2030. Therefore, while blue hydrogen is a key component of the UK's hydrogen strategy, it is essential to manage associated emissions effectively to meet overall climate goals.
